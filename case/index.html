01 · Research Question

When experience cannot be directly shared,
how is understanding negotiated?

We live, more than ever, on other people's experience — reviews, guides, patient stories, the advice of AI. This circulation rests on an assumption we rarely examine: that experience can be spoken, heard, and handed over whole.

Yet understanding always begins from a partial position. We know more than we can tell; language carries only the surface of experience. Understanding was never a handover — it is an ongoing negotiation: guessing, misreading, repairing, until someone says: I think I understand. This project asks how design can reveal, support, or disturb that negotiation — and stages the question with the people for whom it is most unavoidable.

当感知经验无法被直接共享时，设计如何揭示、支持或扰动人们协商理解的过程？

02 · Field Research

Everyone lives on second-hand experience —
and the handover always leaks 实采 N=15+23

Short interviews (N=15) and a bilingual micro-survey (N=23), field-collected in July 2026, asked people not what they believe about understanding but what happened the last time they depended on someone else's experience.

The interviews yielded a taxonomy: understanding does not simply succeed or fail — it ends, in one of six ways.

“I think I understand.”我好像懂了 — the negotiated good-enough ending.
“Forget it.”算了 — the invisible endpoint: giving up on being understood.
Agree to differ求同存异 — negotiating an end to negotiation.
Pretend to understand假装了解 — the social performance of understanding.
Look it up later事后自修 — understanding delayed and privatised.
“As long as you're happy.”祝福性搁置 — ending negotiation with a blessing.
"What honey tastes like — I'd say it's sweet like sugar, but the small part that isn't sugar is usually the part that matters." P13 · the good-enough theory of understanding, from the field 蜂蜜的甜与蔗糖的甜
03 · The Work

Eleven films grown from spoken worlds 原作 · 2024

We asked blind and low-vision friends to describe their worlds, then translated those descriptions into moving images. In the room, the films are the size of a hand; eight voices fill the dark from eight corners; every voice ends on the same sentence.

The captions are the translators' second readings — not quotations. Some films combine the descriptions of several storytellers. They record how we listened, not what was said. 影像旁文字为转译者再读，非讲述者原话。

04 · Exhibition Design

Four halls, one light script 四区 · 一套光线剧本

The exhibition translates the negotiation into a route. Light falls and returns: a dim buffer with the foreword; a blackout star field where eleven films hang at hand-height on stands; a low-lit echo hall with a braille sentence wall, interview footage, and a card you take but cannot read; a bright lounge where the card — and the sentence — are finally read.

06 · The Card

Taken in the dark, read in the light 小卡机制

The exhibition's only takeaway is a card you cannot read where you receive it: braille embossed on the front, ink on the back. Picked up in the dim echo hall, opened in the bright lounge — a pocket-size rehearsal of negotiated understanding.

Alongside it: a braille-embossed ticket, an A6 guidebook with braille pages bound beside ink pages, and the two original neon works placed back into the route — the fractured glyphs at the buffer's exit, the white floor neon at the foot of the braille wall. Braille production passes three gates with blind collaborators: grade decision, proofreading, and tactile sign-off on physical proofs.

07 · Online Exhibition

The films, one at a time 线上展览

A web version rebuilt in web-native language: a typographic index of eleven films with cursor-following previews; each opens into a fullscreen, one-film-at-a-time viewing mode with slow crossfades. The eight voices and the sentence keep their own rooms.
